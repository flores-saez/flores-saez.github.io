Background

The Digital Shift in Social Life

Global internet penetration exceeded 50% for the first time in 2020, with the U.S. rate sitting at approximately 93%. Since 2011, a majority of Americans have reported using social media — and people under 30 adopted these platforms earlier and at higher rates than any other age group, a gap that persists today.

As digital communication has reshaped how people form and maintain relationships, a fundamental question emerges: have these shifts changed how young adults experience belonging and community?

Research Questions & Hypotheses

Two Core Questions

This research addressed two interrelated questions about the relationship between technology use and community identification among young adults in the United States.

Question 1
How strongly does the use of communication technology predict an individual's sense of community?
Hypothesis 1
Technology use frequency is positively associated with reported frequency of feeling belonging within a community.
Question 2
How are demographic disparities in community identification affected by technology use?
Hypothesis 2
The disparities in frequency of reported sense of belonging between white and non-white respondents will decrease with greater technology use.
Data

Panel Study of Income Dynamics — Transition to Adulthood Supplement

The analysis drew on the 2019 wave of the Transition to Adulthood Supplement (TAS) of the Panel Study of Income Dynamics (PSID) — the longest-running longitudinal household study in the world, tracking families since 1968.

The TAS was originally comprised of eligible young-adult children of PSID households drawn from the Child Development Supplement (CDS), beginning in 2005. It was redesigned in 2017 as a biennial survey that expanded to include non-CDS children of PSID households, creating a richer cross-sectional sample of young adults across the country.

The 2019 wave included 2,595 respondents between the ages of 17 and 28. The sample is predominantly white and Black — Black respondents are overrepresented relative to the general population, while other racial minorities are severely underrepresented, a limitation acknowledged in the analysis.

Methods & Findings

Ordered Logistic Regression — Three Model Build

Three sequential models were estimated with community identification frequency as the dependent variable. Model 1 included only technology use frequency. Model 2 added psychological, emotional, and self-esteem well-being measures. Model 3 introduced full demographic and socioeconomic controls. Results are presented as odds ratios.

Key Findings

What the Data Showed

01
Technology use is a significant positive predictor of community identification. In Model 1, the odds ratio of 1.470 (p<.001) indicates that higher technology use frequency is strongly associated with more frequent feelings of community belonging — confirming Hypothesis 1. This effect persists after controlling for well-being and demographics (OR 1.310 in Model 3).
02
White respondents benefit more from technology use than Hispanic and Black respondents. Predictive probability plots show that daily technology users who are white converge toward higher community identification, while Hispanic and Black daily users see smaller gains. Hypothesis 2 was not supported: technology use does not reduce racial disparities in community belonging, and may in fact widen them.
03
Gender disparities narrow with increased technology use. Although females report lower community identification than males overall (OR 0.846, p<.05), the gap between male and female respondents closes substantially as technology use increases. At daily use levels, predictive probabilities for men and women nearly converge.
04
Educational disparities are amplified by technology use. Respondents with some college experience benefit considerably more from technology use (OR 1.631, p<.001) than those without. The gap between college and non-college respondents grows wider at higher use frequencies, suggesting that educational context shapes how digital tools translate into community connection.
05
Psychological and emotional well-being are strong independent predictors. Both psychological well-being (OR 1.407) and emotional well-being (OR 1.329) remain highly significant after full demographic adjustment, suggesting that mental health and community identification are closely intertwined among young adults.
Conclusions

Digital Technology and Community — A Complex Relationship

Digital communication technology has a measurable positive effect on young adults' sense of community belonging. However, the benefits are not evenly distributed. Young people already face structural inequalities in access to community — shaped by race, education, gender, and economic position — and technology use can amplify rather than reduce these divides.

White respondents and those with higher education see the greatest gains from frequent technology engagement. For Hispanic and Black respondents, the relationship is more muted, pointing to the possibility that the social infrastructure through which technology operates — the networks, institutions, and cultural contexts it connects people to — remains unequal.

These findings suggest that expanding digital access alone is insufficient to address disparities in community identification. The quality and character of that access — and the social conditions into which it is embedded — matter as much as frequency of use.
