Background

Loneliness, Community, and the Digital Turn

The U.S. Surgeon General issued an advisory on an "epidemic of loneliness and isolation" in 2023, describing rising levels of social isolation and their implications for public health. Among the contributing factors discussed was the role of social media — a debate that has produced genuinely mixed empirical results. Some studies find that more time on social media is linked to higher perceived isolation; others find that image-based platforms decrease loneliness by raising the sense of social presence.

This project enters that debate with a specific focus on community identification rather than loneliness — a measure that emphasizes an individual's sense of place within formalized social environments, rather than individual subjective experience. The question is whether general digital communication technology use predicts how often young adults feel they belong to a community.

Research Questions & Hypotheses

Two Competing Frameworks

The project is situated within a debate between two well-documented hypotheses of internet use effects. The "rich get richer" hypothesis holds that people with greater social aptitude benefit more from technology use, while more introverted people see worse outcomes. The social compensation hypothesis holds the opposite — that socially anxious individuals prefer digital communication and see net benefits from it.

Hypothesis 1
Increased frequency of digital communication technology use will be positively associated with more frequent reported sense of community belonging, net of all controls.
Hypothesis 2
Technology use will have a positive interaction effect with race and gender, helping people of more marginalized identities reduce existing inequalities in access to community — consistent with the social compensation hypothesis.
Data & Measures

PSID Transition to Adulthood Supplement, 2019

The Panel Study of Income Dynamics (PSID) is the longest-running longitudinal household study in the world, tracking U.S. families since 1968. Its Transition to Adulthood Supplement (TAS) began in 2005 and was redesigned in 2017 to expand eligibility to all children of PSID households, adding technology use questions of direct interest for this analysis.

The 2019 TAS wave (TAS19) includes 2,595 respondents aged 17–28. Black respondents are oversampled at 42.8% of the sample; white and Hispanic respondents are undersampled at 42.35% and 11.69% respectively. PSID 2019 cross-sectional weights were applied throughout.

49.25% of respondents reported earning less than $10,000 in the prior year, consistent with the young-adult transition period the survey is designed to capture.

Key Findings

What the Data Showed

01
Technology use is a consistent, significant positive predictor of community identification across all four models. Beginning at OR 1.650 in Model 1 and settling at OR 1.242 after full controls, the effect is robust — suggesting that higher technology use frequency increases the likelihood of more frequent community belonging by as much as 27% net of all covariates. The only technology use sub-type independently significant was following topics/people online (OR 1.173, p<.01), lending some credence to the social compensation hypothesis.
02
Race is among the strongest predictors in the model. Hispanic respondents are 60% less likely and Black respondents 44% less likely to report more frequent community identification than their white counterparts (Models 3–4). The striking exception is Native Hawaiian and Pacific Islander respondents, who show a 357% greater likelihood — a finding the paper attributes to the more rigidly bounded nature of certain racial communities and the role of shared history and culture in community formation.
03
Higher education enrollment is the single strongest social participation predictor. Respondents who have ever attended college are 215% more likely to report increased community identification frequency — a larger effect than any other variable in the full model, including religious attendance (which at most confers a 56% increase).
04
Age effects disappear once social participation is controlled. Age and age² are both significant in Model 3 but become non-significant in Model 4. This suggests the apparent age decline in community identification is explained by decreasing access to institutionally-backed community structures (sports teams, clubs, schools) as young adults age out of them — not by age itself.
05
No significant interaction terms were found for technology use with race or gender. Hypothesis 2 was not supported. Technology use does not appear to ameliorate demographic inequalities in community identification. The social compensation hypothesis cannot be confirmed — but neither can the "rich get richer" alternative — leaving the mechanism of technology's positive effect an open question.

Conclusions

Structural Barriers, Not Individual Habits

Digital communication technology has a measurable positive effect on young adults' sense of community belonging. But the data tells a more structural story. The strongest predictors of community identification are institutional: college enrollment, organized sports and clubs, religious participation. Technology use matters — but it operates in the context of access to these institutions, which remain deeply unequal.

The racial disparities in community identification — particularly the significant gaps for Hispanic and Black respondents — point toward barriers to public space, housing, and institutional access rather than anything technology can straightforwardly correct. The widening gap between college-educated and non-college-educated young adults is simultaneously a gap in community access, and therefore a gap in health outcomes.

The paper suggests that what may be occurring digitally is a reconstitution of community along new infrastructural lines — paralleling the urbanization process, wherein denser networks of informal ties emerge even as traditional ones weaken. Whether digital community can substitute meaningfully for institutional belonging remains an open and important question.
